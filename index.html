The Effects of the 2010 Citizens United Decision

Citizens United paved the way to developing Political Action Committees, or PACs. These entities served as technically independent funds that were used to heavily assist in campaigns of governmental officials. These PACs are commonly thought to be workarounds to the campaign financing laws already in place, giving corporations greater voices than any one person in the US.
In Summary

To save everyone a bit of time, the result of the Citizens United decision is one of the largest blows to the integrity of American democracy in the last decade. It removed the already faltering legal restraints on megacorporations acting purely for their own benefit, and works in variance with the legal basis it was argued over.

The qualms associated with Citizen United can be traced to popular American distrust in Corporate meddling in our democracy.
For decades, restrictive legislation such as the Tillman Act formed well-respected precedent for quite some time, until a series of legislative and judicial actions created a perfect battleground to breed the eventual court opinion on Citizens United.
What led up to this decision?

Buckley v. Valeo (1976)

A remarkable decision that laid the groundwork for future rulings regarding campaign finances. Upheld limits on contributions from groups outside of candidate himself.

McCain-Feingold Act (2002)

Sponsored by John McCain (R-AZ) and Russ Feingold (D-WI), this Act sought to limit the rise in soft money in all levels in election. More importantly, it limited political advertisements within certain periods of elections.

FEC v. Wisconsin Right to Life, Inc. (2007)

A fundamental case that weakened BCRA provisions. Opened up leeway for appeal of BCRA bans, which became the grounds for which Citizen United was decided.

The 2010 decision stemmed from a political non-profit referred to as Citizen United creating a defamatory film of Hillary Clinton shortly before the 2008 presidential primary elections. The organization was funded by both small donors and massive corporations, so it became legally liable under a variety of laws. The 1971 Federal Election Campaign Act explicitly made it illegal for corporations to use their treasuries to invest in electioneering communication. Knowing their liability, Citizens United sought injunctive relief and was categorically rejected. Then they brought it to the Supreme Court.

The Supreme Court ruled in favor of Citizens United in a 5 to 4 decision, in short agreeing that curtailing their ability to distribute the film Hillary is a violation of their first amendment right. In an opinion written by Justice Kennedy, the Court argued that wealth should not be a delimiting factor to someone’s first amendment right. As such, Citizens United allowed for limitless funding for candidates.

Justice Stevens wrote a particularly lengthy dissent regarding potentially corrupting nature of this decision. Unlimited spending in political campaigns can lead to expanded influence by corporate entities and mitigation of the influence of any common man.
